Design Considerations

The following are some of the issues we ran into throughout the semester while developing our project.

  • Amazon AWS vs Microsoft Azure: The first major issue we faced in our project was choosing a cloud database provider to go with. With the two major options being Amazon AWS and Microsoft Azure, we figured going with one of these systems would probably be our best option. After doing a significant amount of research on the pros and cons of each system, in the end we decided to go with Amazon AWS. AWS offered numerous easy to use "get help" videos and a simplified extra layer of added security needed by our customer.
  • Designing the schema of the database: The second major challenged faced by our team was designing the database. We set about our design using the conventional methods, ER Diagrams and Relational Schemas. During this stage we first met with our customer to talk about what type of data he was looking to collect, and the connections within it. We then developed an ER Diagram and went back and forth with our customer modifying it until we arrived at a final layout he was pleased with. This design continued to progress throughout the semester as new attributes were added and advancements were considered.
  • Part of a Multi-team project: One of the toughest challenges facing our team was simply the overall scale of the project. Throughout the semester our team had to ensure we were in near constant communication with both the desktop and mobile application teams. This, in and of itself, was a challenge as it involved synchronization between over 10 separate people. In the end we had to ensure our product was accessible and functioned correctly as it is the backend that allows both applications to thrive.
  • Connection protocols with mobile application: Connecting our product to desktop was simple as it involved inserting a URL into their code, typical of the way databases are connected to products. However, connecting to the mobile application was a different beast altogether. As mobile developed their product with React Native and other development libraries, our team had to search long and hard for the correct way to make a connection. In the end we solved the problem with creating a separate PHP page on our service to build a connection that React Native could then access.
  • Developing security protocols for the system: As our customer plans to one-day use our design as a baseline for his business, he wanted a sound-proof plan that would allow for security within his product as he will be dealing with confidential information. Our product initiates security using a password at login. This password is then hashed at the application level before being sent to and checked against the database for accuracy. Our team also came up with the idea of Work-Groups which are used to combine users into groups based on the specific cases they are analyzing and can access.
  • Inserting the initial vehicle specs data to the cloud: Another challenge our team faced early in the semester was in uploading a massive vehicle specs file to the cloud. To understand the size of this file, imagine a table with over 30 separate columns, attributes, containing everything from make and model, to number of doors, to the weight and height of the vehicle. Then imagine this file having over 64,000 rows, a row for nearly every car make and model built since the 1970's. Inserting this data and uploading it to the cloud was no simple task. In the end our team solved this issue using MySQL Workbench which actually simplified this process. We found numerous videos demonstrating how initial data could be uploaded which were followed.
  • Developing initial SQL queries used by both desktop and mobile: One of the final issues our team ran into was in developing the initial SQL queries needed for both desktop and mobile. Developing the initial queries themselves wasn't difficult as 3 out 4 team members had taken a database class before. Instead, the difficult part was in testing the queries. As testing our queries involved running them on either the desktop or mobile application against the cloud, this process was slow. Our team had to wait for these other teams to work our queries into their system, and then wait for them to run them and hear back from them. Once we realized this process was not optimal, we decided to build a replica of our database on one of our own personal machines. This allowed queries to be easily developed and de-bugged in house. This simplified the process as we were then able to hand over working queries to both teams.

The following are some of the major changes we had to make to our design during implementation.

  • The addition of new attributes to our schema: As the semester progressed, numerous times we added in new attributes to our database design. Many times our customer either asked for a new field, or we suggested one to him, and it was added in to the proper table.
  • The addition of new tables to our schema: After our team hashed out the Workgroups layer of security and presented it to our customer, we then added in this table to our schema. We also added in the coefficient of friction tables to our reference schema about midway through. As our customer advances his design and product, he will likely continue to add new tables and attributes in the future.

The following are some of the restrictions and limitations to our design.

  • Storing images collected at the scene: As of now, our system is not setup to hold blob data types for picture images. As this data is a crucial piece to the puzzle of analyzing an accident, this will need to be added to the product in the future. Most likely a picture table will be built with two attributes, the image itself, as well as a reference to the case ID the picture was taken at.
  • Limited amount of storage avaliable: As our current system is hosted on AWS's free tier storage, eventually our customer will run out space as the product is used. This is a simple fix as it involves our customer upgrading his storage plan.
  • Connectivity: To connect to our product either the mobile or desktop application must be at a location with access to the internet. As this is the overall idea behind cloud databases, there is nothing that can be done in the future to solve this issue. However, one advancement the mobile team may want to implement is put in place a protocol that allows data to be collected in remote locations without cell service. This protocol would then allow for their app to store the data locally on the device and then upload it to the cloud once in range of a signal.
